# Building the A's Nation in Las Vegas: A Fan's Guide to Being Part of Something Real

The Oakland A's fan base is one of the most loyal, most passionate, and most heartbroken fan communities in professional sports. The relocation to Las Vegas brought a complicated inheritance: grief and anger from the Oakland community, and the challenge of building a new fan base in a city that is still learning what it means to have professional baseball.

This is a guide for Las Vegas A's fans — new ones finding their footing, fans who made the move with the team, and everyone building this community together.

## Why This Moment Matters

Fan cultures are built in the early years. The people who show up at the temporary Las Vegas Ballpark before the permanent stadium opens, who learn the roster names and the players' stories, who bring their kids and make this a family thing — these are the founding generation of Las Vegas A's culture.

The first generation of any franchise's fans in a new city have an outsized influence on who the team becomes and what it means to the community. The Oakland fans who defined A's culture — the Coliseum, the drumming sections, the irreverent connection to a team that was perpetually rebuilding but never without personality — built something that lasted decades. The Las Vegas generation is building something now.

What you do in these early years matters. Show up. Bring people. Make memories. The culture you create now is what your kids will inherit.

## Getting to Know the Roster

The A's 2026 roster has players worth knowing. The young pitchers who came up through the system are where the team's future lives — learning their names now, following their development, knowing their stories before they become stars means you're a real fan rather than a bandwagon.

Start with the rotation. Know who's starting this week and why. Read the beat reporters who cover the team. Baseball is a 162-game season — it rewards fans who pay sustained attention rather than only showing up for big moments.

The position player to watch: whoever the front office has identified as the organizational cornerstone. Every rebuilding team has one. On the A's, it's usually the guy hitting lower in the order right now but generating the best underlying numbers. Find him. Root for him when the results aren't there yet.

## The Las Vegas Ballpark Experience

Las Vegas Ballpark is small by Major League standards. This is genuinely a feature. At 10,000-person capacity, you are closer to the field than you would be at most MLB stadiums. You can hear the infield chatter. You can see the grip on the pitch. You can watch the catcher's signs if you know what you're looking at.

The best seats in the house: sections along the first and third base lines, mid-level, where you get both proximity and angle. The outfield grass sections are worth doing once for the experience — bring sunscreen and know that you're trading sight lines for atmosphere.

**The unwritten rules of Las Vegas Ballpark:**
- Get there early. Parking fills faster than you'd expect.
- The view from the concourse behind home plate during the first inning is excellent for a walk while the crowd settles.
- Twilight games (starting around 7 PM) are the best atmospheric experience in the summer when the heat breaks.
- The food situation has improved. The local vendors have been given more floor space. Use them over the chain options.

## The Fan Community

The Las Vegas A's fan community is building in real-time across multiple platforms. The Reddit communities (/r/LVAthletics and the broader /r/baseball) are where game-day conversations happen. The Twitter/X community has been active and generally good-natured. Discord servers exist for the more engaged fans who want real-time in-game discussion.

For in-person community: the supporter sections — wherever the organized fan groups set up — are where the energy is. Find them. They make road trips to nearby stadiums, they organize watch parties for away games, they have the kind of institutional knowledge about the team that comes from genuine investment.

If you're from Oakland and you made the move: your knowledge and your passion are part of what makes this community real. The Las Vegas A's need people who know what A's baseball culture looks like at its best.

If you're new to baseball and new to the A's: welcome. Seriously. The fan community for this team in this city is actively building its culture and there's no better time to get in than now. Ask questions. People will answer them.

## Making the A's Yours

The best fan relationships with sports teams aren't passive. They involve knowing the team, understanding the game, being present for the good and the bad, and investing enough that the wins feel personal and the losses sting.

That investment is a choice. It requires time and attention. In return, it gives you something that's genuinely hard to find: a community connected by something you all care about, and a source of drama, joy, and occasional heartbreak that is reliably available 162 times a year.

The Las Vegas A's are building something. You can be part of it from the beginning.

That's not a small thing.
